Cities are not static. So why should our stories be?

Cities are about communities. So why don’t we connect with them?

The classic spaces for talking about cities are academic research papers and newspaper reports. But these spaces have failed to adequately innovate with the internet and the potential for conceptualization and execution of research and journalism using new media tools is still not fully realized. Newspapers are definitely making inroads though. Interactive stories are appearing more and more often with increasing levels of sophistication as papers like the New York Times and the Guardian set up in-house labs with this express purpose. For example, consider this story by The Guardian about the contents of the NSA leaks by Edward Snowden . Or this story in The Guardian about a firestorm in Tasmania . For a more data centric example consider the New York Times’ homicide map .

The Urban History Project runs a fellowship program that aims to partner with innovative story-tellers and help them achieve similar levels of refinement with their own projects. To achieve this goal, UHP will take the place of an interactive team or data lab. The fellows will be given to access to our team of editors, designers, data scientists and programmers for the purpose of collaboration. They will also be able to harness the potential of the wider UHP community to crowd-source pertinent aspects of their project.

Apply

The UHP Fellowship seeks to provide enthusiastic individuals with the editorial and technical support needed to execute innovative projects capturing the transformation of our urban spaces.

UHP is interested in all forms of storytelling - audio, video, image, data and text. We expect our fellows to be proficient in at least one of these areas so that they can optimally collaborate with their peers. If you have a complex story to tell about the City and a vision to utilize the potential of new media tools, please apply to be a UHP fellow through the form below.
